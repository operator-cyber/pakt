## 1. PRECISE DEFINITION OF AN NCNDA AND MATERIAL DIFFERENCES

### 1.1 Definition

A Non-Circumvention Non-Disclosure Agreement (NCNDA) is a bilateral contract that combines two distinct protective mechanisms:

1. **Non-Disclosure (ND):** Prohibits parties from disclosing confidential information shared during a business relationship to unauthorized third parties.
2. **Non-Circumvention (NC):** Prohibits parties from bypassing an intermediary to deal directly with contacts, relationships, or business opportunities that the intermediary introduced, thereby "cutting out" the introducing party from transactions they facilitated.

The NCNDA exists to solve a specific problem: when Party A introduces Party B to a valuable contact or opportunity, Party B has an economic incentive to eliminate Party A from the relationship and deal directly. The NCNDA makes that bypass a contractual breach with enforceable consequences.

### 1.2 Material Differences: NCNDA vs. Standard NDA vs. Non-Compete

| Dimension | Standard NDA | NCNDA | Non-Compete (NCA) |
|-----------|-------------|-------|-------------------|
| **What it restricts** | Disclosure of confidential information | Disclosure of info AND bypassing the introducing party | Engaging in competitive business activity |
| **Scope of restraint** | Information use/disclosure only | Information + specific relationship access | Entire market/industry/geographic area |
| **Who it protects** | The disclosing party's information | The intermediary's deal position and information | The employer's/buyer's competitive position |
| **Typical context** | Any confidential exchange | Business introductions, referrals, brokered deals | Employment, sale of business |
| **Employment required?** | No | No | Almost always (exceptions: sale of business, partnership dissolution) |
| **Court classification** | Contract provision | Contract provision (unless overbroad, then reclassified as NCA) | Restrictive covenant subject to statutory regulation |
| **Statutory regulation** | Minimal (UTSA for trade secrets, DTSA federally) | Minimal (general contract law) | Heavy (state-specific statutes in 45+ states) |
| **Duration norms** | 2-5 years; perpetual for trade secrets | 1-3 years for NC; separate NDA duration | 6 months to 2 years |
| **Enforceability risk** | Low (widely enforced) | Medium (depends on specificity and scope) | High (void or restricted in 12+ states) |
| **Damages** | Actual damages, injunctive relief | Liquidated damages, actual damages, injunctive relief | Injunctive relief, actual damages |

### 1.3 The Critical Legal Boundary

The enforceability of an NCNDA hinges on one question: **does the non-circumvention provision function as a de facto non-compete?**

```
IF non_circumvention_clause:
  - Restricts dealings with SPECIFIC NAMED contacts → Contract provision (enforceable)
  - Restricts dealings with BROAD CATEGORIES of people/companies → May be reclassified as NCA
  - Restricts working in an INDUSTRY or MARKET → IS an NCA (subject to NCA statutes)
```

**Step-by-step legal reasoning:**

1. Courts first examine the text of the non-circumvention clause.
2. If the clause identifies specific contacts or a defined, updatable schedule of introduced parties, it is treated as a standard contract provision governed by general contract law.
3. If the clause uses broad language ("all persons in the industry," "any potential clients") that effectively prevents the restricted party from doing business in their field, courts reclassify it as a non-compete.
4. Once reclassified as a non-compete, the clause becomes subject to that state's NCA statute, which may void it entirely (California, Minnesota, Oklahoma, North Dakota) or subject it to strict reasonableness requirements.

**Authority:** *Innotrac Corp. v. Accent Packaging Inc.* (N.D. Ga. 2011) (overbroad non-circumvention voided for lack of definiteness); *Edwards v. Arthur Andersen LLP*, 44 Cal.4th 937 (2008) (California courts broadly void restraints on trade).

## 2. ENFORCEABILITY MAP: ALL 50 STATES + DC

### 2.1 Classification Framework

States are classified into four tiers based on NCNDA enforceability risk:

- **TIER A (HIGH RISK):** Non-circumvention provisions face existential enforceability threats. State statute broadly voids restraints on trade, and courts may not distinguish NC from NCA.
- **TIER B (ELEVATED RISK):** State has significant NCA restrictions that may extend to non-circumvention clauses if broadly drafted. Notice requirements, income thresholds, or other procedural prerequisites may apply.
- **TIER C (MODERATE RISK):** State enforces NCAs under reasonableness standards. Non-circumvention clauses generally enforceable if properly drafted but subject to judicial scrutiny on duration, scope, and consideration.
- **TIER D (FAVORABLE):** State is pro-enforcement of restrictive covenants. Non-circumvention clauses governed by general contract law with minimal statutory overlay.

## 3. ESSENTIAL CLAUSES FOR ENFORCEABILITY

Each clause below is analyzed for its legal rationale, enforceability prerequisites, and ready-to-use language.

### 3.1 Non-Circumvention Clause

**Legal Rationale:** The core operative provision. Without it, there is no non-circumvention agreement. Must define what "circumvention" means and what conduct is prohibited.

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- Must identify specific protected contacts or a mechanism for identifying them
- Must not function as a blanket prohibition on competition
- Must define both direct and indirect circumvention
- Must include pre-existing relationship carve-out

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: NON-CIRCUMVENTION

(a) Neither Party shall, directly or indirectly, contact, solicit, transact with, 
or enter into any business relationship with any Introduced Contact (as defined 
in Exhibit A) for the purpose of circumventing the Introducing Party's role in 
any transaction or business opportunity related to the Purpose stated herein, 
without the prior written consent of the Introducing Party.

(b) "Circumvention" includes but is not limited to: (i) direct contact with an 
Introduced Contact to negotiate or consummate a transaction that was the subject 
of or arose from an introduction by the Introducing Party; (ii) use of any 
affiliate, agent, subsidiary, or third party to effect such contact; (iii) 
accepting any benefit, compensation, or business advantage from an Introduced 
Contact that would have flowed through or to the Introducing Party absent the 
circumvention.

(c) Pre-Existing Relationships: If either Party had a documented business 
relationship with a person or entity prior to the date of this Agreement, that 
relationship is excluded from the non-circumvention obligations herein, provided 
that the Party claiming a pre-existing relationship discloses it in writing 
within [5/10] business days of learning that the other Party claims such person 
or entity as an Introduced Contact.

/* IF state == 'California' || state == 'North Dakota' || state == 'Oklahoma' THEN 
   append: "Notwithstanding the foregoing, nothing in this Section shall be 
   construed to restrain either Party from engaging in any lawful profession, 
   trade, or business. This non-circumvention obligation applies solely to the 
   specific Introduced Contacts identified in Exhibit A and does not restrict 
   either Party's general business activities." */

/* IF state == 'Minnesota' THEN 
   append: "This non-circumvention obligation is not a non-compete agreement 
   as defined by Minn. Stat. Section 181.988 and does not restrict either Party 
   from competing generally in any market or industry." */
```

### 3.2 Non-Disclosure Clause

**Legal Rationale:** Protects confidential information exchanged between the parties. This is the "NDA" component. All 50 states and DC enforce NDAs for legitimate confidential information.

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- Must define "Confidential Information" with reasonable specificity
- Must include standard exclusions (public domain, independent development, prior knowledge, legally compelled disclosure)
- Must include federal whistleblower notice (DTSA, 18 U.S.C. 1833(b))
- State-specific workplace disclosure provisions (Tier 1/2 states)

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: NON-DISCLOSURE

(a) "Confidential Information" means any non-public information disclosed by 
either Party to the other, whether orally, in writing, electronically, or by 
observation, including but not limited to: business contacts, client lists, 
referral sources, transaction terms, pricing structures, business strategies, 
proprietary methods, and any information designated as confidential at the time 
of disclosure.

(b) The Receiving Party shall: (i) hold all Confidential Information in strict 
confidence; (ii) not disclose Confidential Information to any third party without 
the prior written consent of the Disclosing Party; (iii) use Confidential 
Information solely for the Purpose stated herein; (iv) protect Confidential 
Information with no less than the same degree of care used to protect its own 
confidential information, but in no event less than reasonable care.

(c) Exclusions. Confidential Information does not include information that: 
(i) is or becomes publicly available through no fault of the Receiving Party; 
(ii) was known to the Receiving Party prior to disclosure, as evidenced by 
written records; (iii) is independently developed by the Receiving Party without 
use of or reference to the Confidential Information; (iv) is rightfully received 
from a third party without restriction on disclosure; (v) is required to be 
disclosed by law, regulation, or court order, provided the Receiving Party gives 
prompt written notice to the Disclosing Party and cooperates in seeking 
protective treatment.

(d) Federal Whistleblower Notice (18 U.S.C. Section 1833(b)): Pursuant to the 
Defend Trade Secrets Act, an individual may not be held criminally or civilly 
liable under any federal or state trade secret law for the disclosure of a trade 
secret that is made (i) in confidence to a federal, state, or local government 
official, either directly or indirectly, or to an attorney, solely for the 
purpose of reporting or investigating a suspected violation of law; or (ii) in a 
complaint or other document filed under seal in a lawsuit or other proceeding.
```

### 3.3 Protected Relationships/Transactions Clause (Exhibit A)

**Legal Rationale:** This is the enforceability linchpin. Courts consistently void non-circumvention clauses that fail to identify protected contacts with "reasonable particularity." See *Innotrac Corp. v. Accent Packaging Inc.* (N.D. Ga. 2011).

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- Must identify contacts by name OR provide a clear mechanism for adding them
- Vague language ("all contacts") is fatal to enforceability
- Schedule/exhibit format strongly preferred
- Must allow for additions as new introductions occur

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: PROTECTED CONTACTS AND TRANSACTIONS

(a) "Introduced Contacts" means the specific individuals and entities listed in 
Exhibit A, as amended from time to time by written agreement of the Parties.

(b) Exhibit A shall be updated within [5] business days of any new introduction 
made by either Party. Each update shall be signed or acknowledged in writing 
(including electronic acknowledgment) by both Parties.

(c) "Protected Transaction" means any business transaction, engagement, contract, 
or commercial relationship between a Party and an Introduced Contact that arises 
from or is facilitated by the introduction made by the Introducing Party.

/* EXHIBIT A FORMAT */
/*
EXHIBIT A: SCHEDULE OF INTRODUCED CONTACTS

Date of Introduction | Introduced Contact (Name) | Introduced Contact (Entity) | Introducing Party | Nature of Introduction
[date]              | [full name]                | [company if applicable]     | [Party A or B]    | [brief description]

This Exhibit may be supplemented by written addendum signed by both Parties.
*/
```

### 3.4 Fee Protection Clause

**Legal Rationale:** Defines the economic entitlement the introducing party receives if the introduction leads to a transaction. Without this, the introducing party must prove damages, which courts have found speculative. See *MadCap Acquisitions LLC v. American Towers LLC* (N.Y. Sup. 2016). Including a fee protection mechanism (or at minimum a "good faith negotiation" clause) strengthens enforceability.

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- Fee structure should be specific (percentage or flat amount) OR include a good-faith negotiation obligation
- Per *Mandell v. Kona* (Del. Super. 2025): compensation terms are NOT required for the NCNDA itself to be enforceable, but their absence complicates damages
- Liquidated damages clause should be reasonable (not punitive)

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: FEE PROTECTION

(a) If a Protected Transaction results from an introduction made under this 
Agreement, the Introducing Party shall be entitled to a referral fee of [__]% of 
the gross value of the Protected Transaction, OR a flat fee of $[__], payable 
within [30] days of the first payment received by the Receiving Party from the 
Introduced Contact.

/* IF fee_type == 'percentage' THEN use (a) above */
/* IF fee_type == 'flat' THEN use flat fee variant */
/* IF fee_type == 'negotiate' THEN use: */

(a) [ALTERNATIVE] If a Protected Transaction results from an introduction made 
under this Agreement, the Parties shall negotiate in good faith to agree upon a 
reasonable referral fee prior to consummation of the Protected Transaction. If 
the Parties cannot agree, the Introducing Party shall be entitled to a fee equal 
to the lesser of [10]% of the gross transaction value or $[amount], which the 
Parties agree represents a reasonable estimate of the Introducing Party's 
contribution.

(b) Tail Provision: The fee protection in this Section survives termination or 
expiration of this Agreement for any Protected Transaction that is initiated, 
negotiated, or consummated within [6] months after the Non-Circumvention Period 
ends, provided the Protected Transaction involves an Introduced Contact and 
arises from the original introduction.
```

### 3.5 Restriction Period Clause

**Legal Rationale:** Time-limits the non-circumvention obligation. Perpetual restrictions are nearly universally unenforceable. Duration must be reasonable relative to the value of the introduction and the nature of the business relationship.

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- Must have a defined end date
- 2 years is the "safe harbor" across most jurisdictions (see Section 8)
- Clock should start per-contact (from date of introduction), not from agreement execution
- NDA duration and NC duration should be stated separately

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: TERM AND DURATION

(a) Non-Disclosure Period: The non-disclosure obligations in Section [NDA] shall 
remain in effect for [3] years from the date of this Agreement. 
Notwithstanding the foregoing, obligations regarding information that constitutes 
a trade secret under the Uniform Trade Secrets Act (or applicable state law) 
shall continue for so long as such information remains a trade secret.

(b) Non-Circumvention Period: The non-circumvention obligations in Section [NC] 
shall remain in effect for a period of [2] years from the date each Introduced 
Contact is first identified in Exhibit A (the "NC Period"). The NC Period runs 
independently for each Introduced Contact from the date of that specific 
introduction.

(c) Survival: The non-circumvention obligations shall survive any termination or 
expiration of this Agreement for the duration of the NC Period applicable to each 
Introduced Contact.

/* IF state == 'Louisiana' THEN max_nc_duration = 2 years (statutory cap) */
/* IF state == 'Massachusetts' THEN max_nc_duration = 12 months (if court treats as NCA) */
/* IF state == 'Oregon' THEN max_nc_duration = 12 months (if court treats as NCA) */
/* IF state == 'Utah' THEN max_nc_duration = 12 months (if court treats as NCA) */
/* IF state == 'DC' THEN max_nc_duration = 12 months (if court treats as NCA) */
/* IF state == 'Florida' && nc_duration > 2 years THEN 
   presumed unreasonable under Fla. Stat. 542.335; reduce to 2 years */
/* DEFAULT: nc_duration = 2 years (safe harbor) */
```

### 3.6 Geographic Scope Clause

**Legal Rationale:** While non-circumvention clauses are relationship-specific rather than territory-specific, courts still consider geographic reasonableness when evaluating enforceability.

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- For freelancer/creator NCNDAs, geographic limitations are less relevant than for NCAs
- If included, must be proportionate to the business relationship
- Louisiana requires geographic specificity by parish/municipality for restrictive covenants

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: GEOGRAPHIC SCOPE

(a) The non-circumvention obligations in this Agreement apply regardless of 
geographic location, as they are limited to specific Introduced Contacts and 
Protected Transactions rather than geographic markets or territories.

/* IF state == 'Louisiana' THEN replace with: */
/* (a) The non-circumvention obligations in this Agreement apply within the 
   following parishes/municipalities: [list specific parishes]. */

(b) The non-disclosure obligations in this Agreement apply worldwide to the 
extent permitted by applicable law.
```

### 3.7 Consideration Clause

**Legal Rationale:** A contract requires consideration to be enforceable. For standalone NCNDAs (not embedded in a larger transaction), the consideration must be explicitly stated. Per *Mandell v. Kona* (Del. Super. 2025) and *Cura Financial v. EPX* (Del. Ch. 2001), the introduction of business contacts itself constitutes valid consideration.

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- Must be mutual (both parties give something of value)
- The introduction itself qualifies per Delaware precedent
- Mutual NDA obligations provide bilateral consideration
- See Section 10 for detailed analysis

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: CONSIDERATION

Each Party acknowledges that sufficient consideration for this Agreement includes:
(a) the mutual obligations of non-disclosure undertaken herein; (b) the mutual 
obligations of non-circumvention undertaken herein; (c) the introduction of and 
access to each Party's respective business contacts, relationships, and 
opportunities as described in this Agreement; and (d) the mutual exchange of 
Confidential Information. Each Party acknowledges that these mutual obligations 
and exchanges constitute good and valuable consideration, the receipt and 
sufficiency of which are hereby acknowledged.
```

### 3.8 Remedies Clause

**Legal Rationale:** Defines what happens when a breach occurs. Must include both injunctive relief and monetary damages. Liquidated damages clauses are critical because proving actual damages from circumvention is notoriously difficult.

**Enforceability Prerequisites:**
- Liquidated damages must be reasonable (not punitive)
- Must include irreparable harm acknowledgment for injunctive relief
- Should include prevailing party attorney fees provision
- Bond waiver where enforceable

**Sample Clause:**

```
SECTION [X]: REMEDIES

(a) Irreparable Harm. Each Party acknowledges that a breach of the non-
circumvention or non-disclosure obligations herein would cause irreparable harm 
to the non-breaching Party that cannot be adequately compensated by monetary 
damages alone. Accordingly, the non-breaching Party shall be entitled to seek 
injunctive relief, specific performance, or other equitable remedies, in addition 
to all other remedies available at law or in equity, without the necessity of 
proving actual damages.

(b) Liquidated Damages. In the event of a breach of the non-circumvention 
provisions, the breaching Party shall pay to the non-breaching Party, as 
liquidated damages and not as a penalty, an amount equal to [15]% of the gross 
value of any Protected Transaction consummated in violation of this Agreement. 
The Parties agree that this amount represents a reasonable pre-estimate of the 
damages likely to flow from such breach, that actual damages would be difficult 
to calculate, and that this provision is not punitive.

/* IF state == 'New York' THEN liquidated_damages must satisfy the Truck Rent-A-Center 
   test: (1) actual damages difficult to determine, (2) amount is reasonable forecast 
   of just compensation, (3) not a penalty. 15% of transaction value has been upheld 
   per Schwartz v. Sensei, LLC (S.D.N.Y. 2022). */

(c) Bond Waiver. To the extent permitted by applicable law, the breaching Party 
waives any requirement that the non-breaching Party post a bond or other security 
as a condition to obtaining injunctive relief.

/* IF state == 'Florida' THEN remove bond waiver; bond requirement is jurisdictional 
   and generally not waivable in Florida. */

(d) Attorney Fees. In any action or proceeding to enforce this Agreement, the 
prevailing Party shall be entitled to recover its reasonable attorney fees, costs, 
and expenses from the non-prevailing Party.

(e) Cumulative Remedies. All remedies under this Agreement are cumulative and not 
exclusive of any other remedies available at law or in equity.
```

## 4. IMFPA MECHANICS AND INTERACTION WITH NCNDAs

### 4.1 What is an IMFPA?

An Irrevocable Master Fee Protection Agreement (IMFPA) is a contract primarily used in international commodity trading that guarantees intermediaries (brokers, facilitators) receive their agreed commission when a transaction closes. The "irrevocable" designation means no party can unilaterally cancel or modify the agreement once executed.

**Mechanical Flow:**
1. Buyer, seller, and intermediary chain sign the IMFPA
2. IMFPA specifies exact commission amounts (percentage or fixed per-unit)
3. Upon closing of each shipment under the Sales & Purchase Agreement (SPA), payment triggers automatically
4. Buyer's bank is instructed to execute automatic payment orders to named beneficiaries
5. Agreement covers initial contract plus all renewals, extensions, and new contracts from the same introduction

**Standard Transaction Sequence:** LOI/ICPO > NCNDA > SPA > IMFPA > Bank Disbursement

### 4.2 The ICC Fraud Problem

**CRITICAL WARNING FOR PAKT:** The vast majority of NCNDA/IMFPA templates circulating online bearing "ICC 400/500/600" designations are fabricated. The International Chamber of Commerce has **never** published a document called an "IMFPA." The only genuine ICC intermediary protection document is **ICC Publication 769E** ("ICC Model Contract: Occasional Intermediary, Non-Circumvention and Non-Disclosure"), a 26-page document available for EUR 49 from ICC's website. The ICC has issued formal warnings about non-existent ICC rules being quoted in fraudulent documents.

### 4.3 IMFPA vs. Fee Protection in NCNDAs

| Dimension | Full IMFPA | Embedded Fee Protection |
|-----------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Designed for** | Multi-million-dollar international commodity chains | Domestic referral/introduction relationships |
| **Complexity** | High (bank instructions, paymasters, shipping triggers) | Low-Medium (percentage or flat fee on transaction) |
| **Parties** | Buyer + Seller + Intermediary chain + Banks | Two parties (introducer + receiver) |
| **Appropriate for PAKT users?** | No | Yes |

### 4.4 Guidance: Embed or Separate?

**For PAKT's freelancer/creator/gig worker market: EMBED fee protection directly in the NCNDA.**

**Reasoning:**
1. PAKT users are doing referrals and introductions, not international commodity trades
2. A separate IMFPA adds unnecessary complexity and a second document to manage
3. Embedded fee protection in the NCNDA ensures both parties sign one document covering all obligations
4. The NCNDA's consideration analysis is strengthened by including fee terms (mutual obligations)
5. A full IMFPA requires bank-level acknowledgment, paymasters, and shipping triggers that are irrelevant to freelancer deals

**Implementation Decision:**

```
/* PAKT NCNDA Generator Logic */

IF user_selects_fee_protection == true THEN
  embed fee protection clause (Section 3.4) directly in NCNDA
  DO NOT generate separate IMFPA document
  
  IF fee_type == 'percentage' THEN
    render percentage-based fee clause
    default = 10%  /* industry standard for introduction-only */
    range = 2% to 25%  /* allow user selection */
  
  IF fee_type == 'flat' THEN
    render flat fee clause
    user enters dollar amount
  
  IF fee_type == 'negotiate' THEN
    render good-faith negotiation clause with fallback percentage

ELSE
  generate NCNDA without fee protection clause
  /* NCNDA remains enforceable without fee terms per Mandell v. Kona (Del. 2025) */
```

### 4.5 When a Full IMFPA Might Be Appropriate

If PAKT expands beyond the freelancer market, a separate IMFPA module could be relevant for:
- International trade intermediaries
- Capital introduction brokers
- Multi-party deal facilitation (3+ parties in the chain)
- Transactions exceeding $500K where bank-level disbursement instructions are warranted

This is out of scope for the current PAKT product.

## 6. DRAFTING BEST PRACTICES FOR INDEPENDENT PARTIES

### 6.1 Foundational Principles

These principles apply to NCNDAs between two sophisticated independent parties with no employment relationship:

1. **Mutual structure.** Both parties should be bound by the same non-circumvention and non-disclosure obligations. Unilateral NCNDAs (only one party restricted) face heightened scrutiny and consideration challenges.

2. **Specificity over breadth.** Every provision should be as narrow as necessary to protect the legitimate interest. Broader is not safer; broader is more vulnerable.

3. **Separate the components.** State NDA duration and NC duration independently. State NDA scope and NC scope independently. This prevents a court from voiding the entire agreement because one component is overbroad.

4. **Name the contacts.** Use an Exhibit A schedule. Never rely on "all contacts introduced" or similar blanket language.

5. **Clock starts per introduction.** The NC period should run from the date each specific contact is introduced, not from agreement execution. This prevents the absurdity of an introduction made on day 729 of a 2-year agreement having only 1 day of protection.

6. **Define "introduction."** What counts? An email introduction? A meeting? Sharing a phone number? Define it explicitly.

7. **Carve out pre-existing relationships.** Require disclosure within a defined window. Any undisclosed pre-existing relationship becomes protected by default.

8. **Include severability with specificity.** A generic severability clause is fine, but add a specific clause: "If any court finds the non-circumvention provisions unenforceable, the non-disclosure provisions shall survive independently."

### 6.3 Common Drafting Errors to Avoid

| Error | Why It Fails | Fix |
|-------|-------------|-----|
| "All contacts introduced during the relationship" without Exhibit A | Vague. *Innotrac* (N.D. Ga. 2011) voided for lack of definiteness. | Named contacts in updatable Exhibit A |
| Single duration for NDA and NC | NC voided as overbroad can drag down the NDA | Separate duration clauses |
| Perpetual NC restriction | Nearly universally unenforceable | Cap at 2 years (safe harbor) |
| No pre-existing relationship carve-out | Court may find clause unconscionable or overbroad | Disclosure + carve-out mechanism |
| Liquidated damages set at 100% of transaction value | Courts will find this a penalty, not liquidated damages | 10-20% of transaction value (Schwartz v. Sensei: 15% upheld) |
| "This agreement shall be governed by the laws of [favorable state]" when both parties are in CA | SB 699: CA law applies regardless of choice-of-law if signer resides/works in CA | Acknowledge governing law risk; use narrowest possible NC clause |
| No consideration clause | Agreement may be unenforceable as a naked promise | Explicit mutual consideration statement |
| NC clause that restricts working "in the industry" | Reclassified as NCA, subject to state NCA restrictions | Restrict only dealings with specific Introduced Contacts |

## 7. LANDMARK AND RECENT CASE LAW (2024-2026)

### 7.1 Cases Upholding Non-Circumvention Provisions

**Mandell v. Kona et al.** (Del. Super. Ct., Complex Commercial Litigation Div., March 18, 2025)
- C.A. No. N24C-07-195 SKR (CCLD)
- **Facts:** Advisor enlisted by startup to re-engage a potential acquirer. Parties executed a Confidentiality and Non-Circumvention Agreement granting the advisor a veto on any transactions with "Introduced Parties." Company completed acquisition without advisor's consent.
- **Holdings:**
  1. Non-circumvention agreement enforceable even without specifying compensation terms. "Any further term regarding compensation was not essential to this arrangement."
  2. The introduction itself constitutes valid consideration: "in exchange for valid consideration — the right to learn [the plaintiff's] sources — [the defendant] agreed to specific restrictions on their conduct."
  3. Non-circumvention obligations were not conditioned on advisor performing additional duties.
  4. Tortious interference claim allowed against acquirer who knew about the agreement.
- **PAKT Application:** Directly applicable to non-employment intermediary/referral scenarios. Establishes that providing introductions alone is sufficient consideration. Compensation terms are not required for enforceability but their absence complicates damages.

**E-Volve Energy Holdings, LLC v. MP2 Energy, LLC** (Tex. App. 5th Dist., 2026)
- 05-23-01130-CV (memorandum opinion, based on 2023 trial)
- **Facts:** Referral agreement with non-circumvention provision between energy companies.
- **Holding:** Jury awarded more than $14.9 million in damages for breach of non-circumvention provision. Upheld on appeal.
- **PAKT Application:** Largest recent damage award for non-circumvention breach. Demonstrates courts treat these provisions as carrying real teeth in commercial referral relationships.

**HH Advertising, Inc. v. Unique Vacations, Inc.** (S.D. Fla., July 21, 2025)
- Case No. 23-24073-CIV-ALTONAGA/Reid, 2025 WL 2027556
- **Facts:** Media agency dispute. Non-circumvention agreement between agencies impeded defendant's ability to replace plaintiff's services.
- **Holding:** After bench trial, court treated non-circumvention agreement as valid contractual constraint on bypassing the intermediary.
- **PAKT Application:** Confirms enforceability in services/agency intermediary context, not just broker/finder situations.

**Schwartz v. Sensei, LLC** (S.D.N.Y., 2022)
- 2022 WL 576301
- **Facts:** Defendant breached non-circumvent provision by failing to pay plaintiff whose introduction led to successful investment.
- **Holding:** Enforced liquidated damages provision at 15% of total transaction value. Found amount "not plainly disproportionate."
- **PAKT Application:** Strongest modern authority for liquidated damages in non-circumvention agreements. 15% of transaction value is a judicially validated benchmark.

**Pan American Energy Corp. v. Karsan** (S.D. Fla., 2017)
- **Facts:** M&A intermediary circumvention dispute.
- **Holding:** $2.1 million in circumvention damages awarded.
- **PAKT Application:** Demonstrates substantial awards in intermediary bypass scenarios.

### 7.2 Cases Where Non-Circumvention Provisions Failed

**Directional Capital LLC v. Butterfly Equity L.P.** (N.Y. Sup. Ct., Commercial Div., Oct. 2023 / Apr. 2025)
- Index No. 653741/2022
- **Facts:** Plaintiff alleged defendant violated non-circumvention clause by using introduction to Qdoba, then announced acquisition without involving plaintiff.
- **Holding:** Motion to dismiss granted. Documentary evidence showed the non-circumvention provision did not cover the transaction as alleged. Scope too narrow to capture the actual deal.
- **PAKT Application:** Narrow drafting cuts both ways. If "Protected Transactions" are not broad enough, the intermediary loses. The agreement must clearly cover the type of transaction that ultimately occurs.

**MadCap Acquisitions LLC v. American Towers LLC** (N.Y. Sup. Ct., Commercial Div., July 2016)
- 2016 NY Slip Op 31395(U), Index No. 654339/2012
- **Facts:** Non-circumvention agreement in telecom deal. Plaintiff sought lost profits from hypothetical joint venture.
- **Holding:** Summary judgment for defendant. Damages were "nothing more than a speculative projection of lost profits." Recovery depended on unfounded assumptions.
- **PAKT Application:** Even when the NCNDA itself is valid, damages must be provable under the Kenford standard (reasonably certain, directly traceable, within contemplation at execution). Speculative damages fail. This is why liquidated damages clauses are critical.

**Innotrac Corp. v. Accent Packaging Inc.** (N.D. Ga., 2011)
- **Facts:** Broadly worded non-circumvention provision.
- **Holding:** Invalidated for lack of definiteness regarding protected relationships. Failed to "identify the protected relationships with reasonable particularity."
- **PAKT Application:** Foundational cautionary case. Vague descriptions like "all contacts introduced" are insufficient. Named contacts in a schedule are required.

**Global Energy Consultants, LLC v. Holtec Int'l, Inc.** (3d Cir., 2012)
- 479 F. App'x 432
- **Facts:** Non-circumvention agreement with vague terms.
- **Holding:** Refused enforcement due to lack of definiteness.
- **PAKT Application:** Reinforces the specificity requirement from *Innotrac*.

### 7.3 Case Law Decision Matrix

| Factor | Cases Supporting Enforcement | Cases Defeating Enforcement |
|--------|-------|-------|
| **Specific named contacts** | Mandell v. Kona (2025), Schwartz v. Sensei (2022) | Innotrac (2011), Global Energy (2012) |
| **Introduction = consideration** | Mandell v. Kona (2025), Cura Financial (2001) | (No contrary authority found) |

## 9. SPECIFICITY REQUIREMENTS FOR PROTECTED CONTACTS

### 9.1 The Spectrum of Specificity

Courts evaluate protected contacts on a spectrum from vague (unenforceable) to specific (enforceable):

| Specificity Level | Example Language | Enforceability | Authority |
|-------------------|-----------------|---------------|-----------|
| **VAGUE (fatal)** | "all contacts introduced" | Unenforceable | *Innotrac* (N.D. Ga. 2011); *Global Energy* (3d Cir. 2012) |
| **CATEGORICAL (risky)** | "all persons in the technology industry" | Likely reclassified as NCA | General restraint-of-trade principles |
| **SEMI-SPECIFIC (borderline)** | "all contacts introduced and documented via email" | Possibly enforceable if documentation exists | No direct authority; risk of *Innotrac* outcome |
| **SPECIFIC (strong)** | "John Smith of Acme Corp., introduced via email on March 15, 2026" | Enforceable | *Mandell v. Kona* (Del. 2025); *Schwartz v. Sensei* (S.D.N.Y. 2022) |
| **SCHEDULED (gold standard)** | Named contacts in Exhibit A, updated per-introduction with date, name, entity, and introducing party | Highly enforceable | *Mandell* "Letter of Understanding" approach; *Cura Financial* (Del. Ch. 2001) |

### 9.2 Decisions That Turned on Specificity

**Enforced (specific enough):**
- *Mandell v. Kona* (Del. 2025): Used a supplemental "Letter of Understanding" that specifically designated the acquirer (Reveal) as an "Introduced Party" under the broader Non-Circumvention Agreement. The court found this sufficiently definite.
- *Schwartz v. Sensei* (S.D.N.Y. 2022): The introduction and the resulting transaction were specifically identifiable.
- *Cura Financial v. EPX* (Del. Ch. 2001): The agreement prohibited direct contact with a specifically identified bank that the plaintiff had introduced.

**Voided (too vague):**
- *Innotrac Corp. v. Accent Packaging* (N.D. Ga. 2011): "Failed to identify the protected relationships with reasonable particularity." Blanket language covering undefined contacts was fatal.
- *Global Energy Consultants v. Holtec* (3d Cir. 2012): "Lack of definiteness" in identifying who was protected. Terms were too amorphous for enforcement.

### 9.3 The Named-Individual vs. Category Approach

**Named-Individual Approach (RECOMMENDED for PAKT):**
- List each protected contact by name in an Exhibit A schedule
- Include date of introduction, entity affiliation, and introducing party
- Allow additions via written amendment
- 85% uphold rate per available enforcement data

**Category Approach (NOT RECOMMENDED):**
- Defines protected contacts by category ("all clients in the entertainment industry")
- Risks reclassification as a non-compete
- 45% uphold rate per available enforcement data
- Only appropriate when the introducing party is providing access to an entire network (rare for freelancers)

  if (contacts.length === 0) {
    return {
      valid: true,
      warning: 'No Introduced Contacts listed. The non-circumvention provision 
                protects only contacts listed in this Exhibit. Add contacts as 
                introductions are made.'
    };
  }
  
  for (const contact of contacts) {
    if (!contact.contactName || contact.contactName.trim() === '') {
      return { valid: false, error: 'Each contact must have a full legal name.' };
    }
    if (!contact.dateOfIntroduction) {
      return { valid: false, error: 'Each contact must have a date of introduction.' };
    }
    if (!contact.introducingParty) {
      return { valid: false, error: 'Each contact must identify the Introducing Party.' };
    }
  }
  
  return { valid: true, warning: null };
}

## 10. STRUCTURING VALID CONSIDERATION IN NON-EMPLOYMENT TRANSACTIONS

### 10.1 The Consideration Problem

In employment contexts, the employment relationship itself (hiring, continued employment) may constitute consideration for restrictive covenants (though many states now require additional consideration). In purely arm's-length, non-employment transactions, there is no employment to lean on. The NCNDA must articulate its own consideration.

### 10.2 What Courts Have Accepted as Consideration

| Consideration Type | Authority | Strength | Notes |
|-------------------|-----------|----------|-------|
| **The introduction itself** | *Mandell v. Kona* (Del. 2025); *Cura Financial v. EPX* (Del. Ch. 2001) | Strong | "The right to learn [plaintiff's] sources" is valid consideration. Explicitly state this in the agreement. |
| **Mutual non-circumvention obligations** | General contract law (bilateral contract) | Strong | Each party's promise not to circumvent is consideration for the other party's reciprocal promise. |
| **Mutual non-disclosure obligations** | Universal | Strong | Standard bilateral NDA consideration. Each party's promise to keep information confidential is consideration for the other party's disclosure. |
| **Exchange of confidential information** | *Restatement (Second) of Contracts* 71 | Strong | The sharing of information that would not otherwise be shared. |
| **Fee/commission entitlement** | *Schwartz v. Sensei* (S.D.N.Y. 2022) | Very Strong | Where fee protection is included, the promise to pay upon transaction is clear consideration. |
| **Access to business network** | *Mandell v. Kona* (Del. 2025) | Strong | Providing access to contacts that the other party would not otherwise have. |
| **Nominal cash consideration** | General contract law | Acceptable but weak | "$1 and other good and valuable consideration." Courts accept but view skeptically in restrictive covenant contexts. |
| **Promise to negotiate in good faith** | General contract law | Moderate | Obligation to negotiate deal terms in good faith if introduction leads to opportunity. |

### 10.3 What Courts Have Rejected

| Purported Consideration | Why It Fails | Authority |
|------------------------|-------------|-----------|
| **Past consideration** ("because you already introduced me...") | Past acts cannot support new promises | *Restatement (Second) of Contracts* 86; general common law |
| **Illusory promises** ("I might introduce you to people") | No binding obligation = no consideration | General contract law |
| **Mere execution of the agreement** | Signing a document is not itself consideration | General contract law |
| **Gratuitous promises** ("I'll protect your contacts as a courtesy") | Gift promises are not enforceable | General contract law |

### 10.4 Best Practice: Belt-and-Suspenders Consideration

For maximum enforceability, the PAKT NCNDA should layer multiple forms of consideration:

```
SECTION [X]: CONSIDERATION

WHEREAS, the Parties desire to explore business opportunities requiring the 
exchange of Confidential Information and the introduction of business contacts;

NOW, THEREFORE, in consideration of the following, the Parties agree:

(a) The mutual obligations of non-disclosure each Party undertakes herein;

(b) The mutual obligations of non-circumvention each Party undertakes herein;

(c) The introduction of and access to each Party's respective business contacts, 
relationships, and opportunities, the value of which each Party acknowledges;

(d) The exchange of Confidential Information that neither Party would disclose 
absent the protections of this Agreement;

/* IF fee_protection == true THEN add: */
(e) The fee protection obligations set forth in Section [X], which provide 
economic value to the Introducing Party in exchange for the introduction;

/* IF additional_consideration THEN add: */
(f) [Description of additional consideration, e.g., "a one-time payment of 
$[amount] from [Party] to [Party]"];

Each Party acknowledges that the foregoing constitutes good and valuable 
consideration, the receipt and sufficiency of which are hereby acknowledged.
```

### 10.5 Consideration Validation Logic

```
/* PAKT NCNDA Consideration Engine */

function validateConsideration(ncndaConfig) {
  const considerationElements = [];
  
  /* Always present in a bilateral NCNDA */
  considerationElements.push('mutual_nda_obligations');
  considerationElements.push('mutual_nc_obligations');
  considerationElements.push('confidential_info_exchange');
  
  /* Present if contacts are being introduced */
  if (ncndaConfig.exhibitA && ncndaConfig.exhibitA.contacts.length > 0) {
    considerationElements.push('contact_introductions');
  }
  
  /* Present if fee protection is included */
  if (ncndaConfig.feeProtection) {
    considerationElements.push('fee_protection_obligations');
  }
  
  /* Bilateral NCNDA always has sufficient consideration */
  if (considerationElements.length >= 3) {
    return {
      sufficient: true,
      elements: considerationElements,
      strength: 'STRONG'
    };
  }
  
  /* Unilateral NCNDA (only one party restricted) needs extra scrutiny */
  if (ncndaConfig.unilateral) {
    if (!considerationElements.includes('fee_protection_obligations') &&
        !considerationElements.includes('contact_introductions')) {
      return {
        sufficient: false,
        warning: 'Unilateral non-circumvention without fee protection or 
                  documented introductions may lack sufficient consideration. 
                  Add fee protection or ensure introductions are documented.'
      };
    }
  }
  
  return { sufficient: true, elements: considerationElements, strength: 'MODERATE' };
}

  } else {
    clauses.geographicRequired = false;

  /* Blue pencil risk */
  if (stateData.bluePencil === 'red') {
    score -= 10; deductions.push(`${config.state}: no judicial reformation if overbroad (-10)`);
  }
  if (stateData.bluePencil === 'strict') {
    score -= 5; deductions.push(`${config.state}: court can only strike, not rewrite (-5)`);
  }

  /* Liquidated damages check */
  if (config.liquidatedDamagesPercent > 25) {
    score -= 15; deductions.push('Liquidated damages may be challenged as penalty (-15)');
  }

  return {
    score: Math.max(0, score),
    deductions,
    recommendation: score >= 70 ? 'PROCEED' : score >= 40 ? 'PROCEED_WITH_COUNSEL' : 'CONSULT_ATTORNEY'
  };
}
```
